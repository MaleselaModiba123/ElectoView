# Stakeholders.md - ElectroView: Electircity Usage Analytics dashboard

---
## 1. Overview

In this document the key stakeholders of the ElectroView system have been identified, their roles, concerns, pain points, and measurable success metrics.
 
---

## 2. Stakeholder Analysis Table

| # | Stakeholder | Role | Key Concerns | Pain Points | Success Metrics |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Electricty Distribution Manager** | Oversees the electricity distribution network for a municipality. Responsible for grid reliability, demand management, and operational reporting. | Real-time visibility into consumption across all distribution zones; early detection of faults and overloads; accurate demand forecasting to prevent outages. | Currently relies on delayed, manually compiled reports. No centralised dashboard to monitor all zones simultaneously. Fault detection is reactive — reported by consumers rather than detected proactively. | Reduce fault detection time from hours to under 15 minutes. Achieve 95% uptime of the monitoring dashboard. Reduce unplanned outages by 20% within 6 months of deployment. |
| 2 | **Electrcity Network Analyst** | Technical staff member responsible for analysing consumption patterns, identifying inefficiencies, and producing reports for planning and infrastructure investment decisions. | Access to historical consumption trends broken down by zone, time of day, and season. Ability to export clean datasets for external analysis. Anomaly logs for post-incident review. | Data is currently spread across disconnected spreadsheets and legacy systems. No unified tool for trend analysis. Report generation is manual and time-consuming, often taking a full working day. | Reduce report generation time from 8 hours to under 30 minutes. Achieve 100% data traceability between anomaly events and meter records. Increase analytical output by enabling weekly instead of monthly reporting. |
| 3 | **Consumer** | An individual household connected to the municipal grid. Uses the system to monitor their own electricity usage, understand their consumption habits, and manage costs. | Clear, understandable view of their personal usage history. Comparison between billing periods. Alerts when usage is unusually high so they can investigate. | Currently receives only a monthly bill with no breakdown of when or where consumption occurred. Cannot self-diagnose whether their meter is faulty. Have no way to set usage budgets or get proactive alerts. | 70% of users can independently interpret their usage chart within one session. 50% reduction in consumer billing dispute queries to the utility call centre within 3 months. Average session duration under 3 minutes for routine usage checks. |
| 4 | **System Administrator** | Internal IT staff responsible for deploying, configuring, and maintaining the ElectroView platform. Manages user accounts, system integrations, server health, and security configurations. | System stability and uptime. Simple deployment process with clear documentation. Ability to manage user roles and permissions without developer intervention. Secure access control and audit logs. | Existing utility systems are difficult to maintain due to poor documentation and tightly coupled architectures. User account management requires direct database access. No centralised audit trail for who accessed or modified what. | System deployable in under 2 hours using provided Docker setup. 99.5% monthly uptime achieved. Zero critical security incidents in the first year of production. All user access changes logged and reviewable. |
| 5 | **Finance Department Staff** | Finance staff responsible for validating electricity bills, handling consumer disputes, and reconciling metered consumption data with invoiced amounts. | Accurate, auditable consumption data per meter per billing period. Easy access to historical readings to resolve disputes. Exportable reports in formats compatible with billing software. | Currently cross-references paper meter readings with billing system manually. Disputes require going back through multiple disconnected records. Export formats are incompatible with financial systems, requiring manual re-entry. | Reduce billing dispute resolution time from 5 days to under 1 day. Achieve 100% traceability of billed consumption back to timestamped meter readings. Reports exportable in CSV format compatible with existing SAP billing system. |
| 6 | **Electrcity Technician** | On-the-ground technical staff who install, maintain, and replace smart meters. Uses the system to identify meters that are malfunctioning, generating anomalous readings, or have gone offline. | Clear list of meters flagged as anomalous or offline, sortable by zone. Ability to update meter status after a physical inspection or replacement. Accurate location data to prioritise field visits efficiently. | Currently receives fault lists via phone or email with no structured tracking system. No way to log the outcome of a field visit back into the system. Duplication of effort when two technicians are assigned to the same fault. | Reduce average time from anomaly detection to field resolution from 48 hours to under 12 hours. 100% of resolved anomalies logged with resolution notes in the system. Technicians able to identify their assigned faults within 30 seconds of login. |
| 7 | **Municipal Executive** | Senior management who requires high-level summaries of grid performance, infrastructure utilisation, and consumption trends to support strategic and budgetary decisions. | High-level KPI dashboards (total consumption, revenue indicators, infrastructure load). Trend reports for infrastructure investment planning. Evidence of system ROI. Does not need raw data — needs meaningful summaries. | Currently relies on analysts to compile ad-hoc PowerPoint summaries. No self-service reporting capability. Data arrives weeks after the period it covers, limiting its usefulness for time-sensitive decisions. | Executives can independently access a summary dashboard without analyst support within one week of onboarding. Monthly executive summary report auto-generated and available on the 1st of each month. Infrastructure utilisation data available with ≤24-hour lag. |
